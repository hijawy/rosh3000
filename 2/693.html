Which of the following is an indication to discontinue the patient's home insulin pump upon hospital admission of a patient with type 1 diabetes?

  • AGlucocorticoid therapy
  • BHyperglycemia
  • CKetoacidosis
  • DNothing by mouth status










































Correct Answer ( C )

Explanation:

Patients with type 1 diabetes mellitus sometimes use an insulin pump for glucose control. An insulin pump is a wearable device that delivers continuous subcutaneous short-acting insulin at a basal rate, which may be supplemented with bolus doses after meals and as needed. The pump has a reservoir that houses the short-acting insulin and may attach to a replaceable subcutaneous cannula directly or through tubing. The pump may monitor interstitial blood glucose using a subcutaneous sensor, or the patient may be required to monitor it separately with traditional finger sticks to obtain capillary blood. Note that a pump that monitors interstitial glucose has a 10-minute lag time, and those values cannot be used for monitoring in an acute setting. Patients using an insulin pump may develop ketoacidosis very quickly if the pump is discontinued, often in less than an hour. Discontinuation is rarely indicated. If the pump needs to be discontinued for a procedure such as an MRI, then give the patient a dose of short- or long-acting insulin. If a patient using an insulin pump develops diabetic ketoacidosis, the clinician should assume that the pump has failed. The pump malfunction can actually be contributing to ketoacidosis. Therefore, the clinician should discontinue it and begin an IV infusion of short-acting insulin. Pump failure may be due to a kink in the tubing, an empty reservoir, disconnection, mechanical malfunction, or errors in priming the pump. While most monitors have alarms in these settings, older models may not. Other reasons that an insulin pump should be discontinued upon hospital admission include an impaired level of consciousness, suicidal ideation, critical illness requiring intensive care, and patient inability or unwillingness to participate in diabetes care.



Patients on glucocorticoid therapy (A) are expected to experience hyperglycemia, which can be managed with frequent blood glucose checks and extra bolus doses of insulin as needed. Hyperglycemia (B) is treated the same in type 1 diabetes mellitus with an insulin pump as with other patients. Do not discontinue the insulin pump because ketoacidosis can develop quickly. It is recommended that a patient who is in nothing by mouth (NPO) status (D) check their blood glucose every 30 to 60 minutes and reduce the basal rate if hypoglycemia occurs. Do not discontinue the insulin pump, as ketoacidosis may occur rapidly.

Question:

True or false: an insulin pump is ideally suited for a patient who does not regularly adhere to their medication regimen, has little understanding of their condition, and is not proactive in the management of blood glucose levels.

False.

Brugada Syndrome

  • Autosomal dominant
  • Cardiac ion channel dysfunction associated with sudden cardiac arrest
  • Structurally normal heart
  • Diagnosis requires characteristic ECG and clinical findings
  • ECG with RBBB-like pattern
    • Type 1: coved ST elevation > 2 mm in at least 2 leads (V1–V3) followed by negative T wave (Brugada sign)
    • Type 2: saddleback ST elevation > 2 mm followed by upright or biphasic T wave
    • Type 3: coved or saddleback ST elevation < 2 mm
    • Type 2 and 3 changes are nondiagnostic and require further workup
  • Clinical
    • Vfib or Vtach
    • FH sudden cardiac death at < 45 years
    • Coved ST elevation in ECG of family members
    • Indicible Vtach
    • Syncope
    • Nocturnal agonal respiration
  • Treatment: ICD

Targeted Temperature Management

The TTM2 trial (2021) suggests that maintaining normothermia is at least equivalent to targeted hypothermia.
